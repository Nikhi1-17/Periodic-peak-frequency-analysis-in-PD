This project focused on characterizing the natural frequency of cortical regions. The natural frequency is defined as the neural oscillation that contributes most strongly to the EEG power spectral density (PSD) following transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS). This metric reflects the intrinsic oscillatory response of a cortical region to external perturbation.
The pipeline laid special emphasis on induced power spectral data. It was computed by subtracting the evoked response from individual trials (evoked response refers to the time series response that is consistent across trials). Power spectra were then estimated and averaged across trials. To robustly isolate physiologically meaningful oscillations, the spectra were decomposed into periodic and aperiodic components, and the frequency exhibiting maximal periodic power was identified as the region’s natural frequency. Unlike earlier approaches based on Morlet wavelets and baseline comparisons, this framework more effectively captured contributions from lower-frequency oscillations and leveraged longer recording durations. The entire analysis pipeline was implemented from scratch in MATLAB. A parallel pipeline was also applied to spectra combining both induced and evoked responses for comparison.
This framework was applied to a dataset comprising healthy controls, Parkinson’s disease (PD) patients ON medication, and PD patients OFF medication. We extracted features from both induced-only spectra and combined- induced + evoked spectra to assess their sensitivity to disease state. These key features included the natural frequency itself, the relative power contribution at the natural frequency and the aperiodic exponent. Our analysis demonstrated that features derived from induced activity were stronger predictors of parkinsonian state, reliably differentiating PD patients from healthy controls regardless of medication status. In contrast, features derived from combined induced + evoked spectra showed reduced sensitivity to disease-related differences.
